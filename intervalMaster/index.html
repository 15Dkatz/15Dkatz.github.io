The Music Theory of Intervals

You can measure intervals by using half-steps on the keyboard.

Major & Perfect Intervals
A major second (M2) consists of two half-steps.
For example, C to D is a major second because it starts with C, goes a half-step up to C#, and one more half-step up to D.
A major third (M3) consists of four half-steps. For example, C to E.
A perfect fourth (P4) consists of five half-steps. For example, C to F.
A perfect fifth (P5) consists of seven half-steps. For example, C to G.
A major sixth (M6) consists of nine half-steps. For example, C to A.
A major seventh (M7) consists of eleven half-steps. For example, C to B.
A perfect eighth (P8) or perfect octave consists of 12 half-steps. For example, C to C.

The musical terms major and perfect refer to the interval's quality.
Minor Intervals
Minor intervals have one less half step than a major interval.

A minor third (m2) consists of one half-step. For example, C to C#.
A minor third (m3) consists of three half-steps. For example, C to Eb or (D#).
A minor sixth (m6) consists of eight half-steps. For example, C to Ab or (G#).
A minor seventh (m7) consists of ten half-steps. For example, C to Bb or (A#).
Augmented & Diminished Intervals
An augmented interval has one more half step than a perfect one.
The augmented fourth (A4) or tritone consists of six half-steps. For example, C to F#.

A diminished interval has one less half step than a perfect one.
The diminished fifth (D5) also the tritone consists of six half-steps. For example, C to Gb or (F#).
